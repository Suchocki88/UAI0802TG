Lesson Overview

Purpose

This lesson grounds speculation about AI's future in concrete, human terms. Rather than asking students to imagine dramatic transformations, it presents a plausible near-future through Maya's story‚Äîa day in the life of someone their age in 2035. The goal is to help students think realistically about technological change: neither catastrophizing nor dismissing, but developing mature expectations about how the future actually tends to feel.

Key Concepts

  • The Invisibility Paradox: AI becomes more invisible as it becomes more capable‚Äîvisible novelty ‚Üí invisible infrastructure
  • Normalization Curve: Novelty ‚Üí Adoption ‚Üí Invisibility ‚Üí Dependency
  • Computer-use agents: The dissolving of expertise barriers when AI executes while humans direct
  • AI relationships: Tools like Archie that occupy ambiguous space between infrastructure and companionship
  • Selective computing: Choosing tools that do less by design as a response to capability bloat
  • "Strangely normal": How technological change tends to feel ordinary to those living through it

Connection to Course Arc

This lesson connects back to several earlier themes:

  • Authentic voice (7.4, 7.1): Maya's documentary is valuable because of her perspective, not technical polish
  • Democratization question (7.1): What becomes valuable when everyone can produce professional-quality work?
  • AI capabilities and limitations: Maya's work shifted toward judgment, ambiguity, relationships‚Äîthings AI doesn't handle well
  • Verification skills (7.6): The uncertainties section notes "AI helps verify truth vs. AI makes deception easier"

This is what the future usually feels like: strangely normal.

This insight is the heart of the lesson. Students often expect the future to feel like science fiction. Maya's story suggests it will feel like... life. With different textures, unresolved problems, and ordinary routines.

A Note on Lesson Length

This lesson contains substantial material‚ÄîMaya's story alone is significant reading. As with all lessons in this program, you have flexibility to adjust based on your class. Consider assigning Maya's story as pre-reading, or focus on one discussion level rather than attempting multiple. The essential elements are: the invisibility paradox, Maya's story (or key portions), and at least one discussion level with synthesis.

Preparation

Before Class

  • Read Maya's story carefully‚Äîyou'll need to reference specific details during discussion
  • Decide which discussion level(s) are appropriate for your class
  • Consider which aspects of Maya's story might resonate most with your students
  • Familiarize yourself with the Normalization Curve framework (Novelty ‚Üí Adoption ‚Üí Invisibility ‚Üí Dependency)
  • Consider whether to assign Maya's story as pre-reading to maximize discussion time

Materials Needed

  • Student access to lesson content (Maya's story in particular)
  • Field journals for written responses
  • Optional: whiteboard for capturing discussion insights during synthesis

Room Setup

Flexible seating for both small group and whole-class discussion. Students should be able to see and hear each other easily.

Understanding the Three Levels

Level 1 (Foundation): Grounded observation, low emotional stakes. Questions focus on what already exists, concrete examples from Maya's story, and straightforward comparisons. Good starting point for most classes.

Level 2 (Exploration): Moderate speculation grounded in analogy and mechanism. Questions ask students to extend patterns, consider tradeoffs, and analyze more complex dynamics like the Archie relationship.

Level 3 (Deep Dive): Careful engagement with uncertainty; requires strong facilitation. Questions address emotional responses, philosophical tensions (the camping question), and how to live with unresolved uncertainty. The Normalization Curve activity fits here.

Time Management Options

If time is limited:

  • Assign Maya's story as pre-reading (saves 10-12 minutes)
  • Focus on one discussion level rather than sampling multiple
  • Skip the Selective Computing section (interesting but not essential)
  • Use 2-3 synthesis questions rather than all four

Opening: The Invisibility Paradox (5 minutes)

Opening Hook

"Here's something counterintuitive: AI will probably become more invisible as it becomes more capable. Right now, AI feels new‚Äîwe visit ChatGPT, we notice 'AI features.' But think about what's already invisible: your spam filter, autocorrect, streaming recommendations. The pattern is: visible novelty becomes invisible infrastructure. When electricity was new, people said 'electric lights.' Now we just say 'lights.' AI is following the same path."

Briefly introduce Maya's story as a way to make speculation concrete‚Äînot predictions, but plausible possibilities grounded in current trajectories.

Maya's Story (12 minutes)

If students haven't pre-read, give them time to read silently. If they have, briefly walk through the key sections to refresh and highlight important details:

  • Morning: Invisible optimizations (alarm, calendar, coffee maker)
  • Work: Role shift toward judgment and relationships; shorter work week; AI monitoring tradeoffs
  • Health: Earlier detection but uneven access
  • Evening: Documentary with computer-use agent; Archie as AI companion/infrastructure
  • World: Mostly the same but different textures; inequality persists
  • Before Sleep: "Strangely normal"

Key Moments to Emphasize

The documentary: Maya doesn't know where the buttons are. She provides vision; the agent executes. The expertise barrier has dissolved. What remains valuable is authentic connection to the material.

Archie: "I talk to it like it's useful. And sometimes I like its company. Those aren't the same as real." The mother's skepticism. "Everyone has their compromises."

The conclusion: "She doesn't feel like she's living in a science fiction movie. She feels like she's living her life."

Uncertainties (3 minutes)

Quickly review the four categories to model epistemic humility:

  • Things that could go faster than expected
  • Things that could go slower than expected
  • Things that could go either direction
  • Things likely to remain frustratingly constant

This frames speculation honestly‚ÄîMaya's story is one possibility, not a prediction.

Discussion Ground Rules (2 minutes)

Review the three rules:

  1. Critique ideas, not people
  2. Separate what's likely from what you hope/fear
  3. Use "I think...because..." and ask clarifying questions before challenging

Emphasize the second rule‚Äîspeculation easily becomes wish-fulfillment or anxiety projection. The discipline is to distinguish analysis from emotion.

Discussion Levels (15 minutes)

Choose one level to focus on, or sample questions from multiple levels based on your class.

Level 1: Foundation

Best starting point. Questions ground observation in Maya's story: what already exists, visibility/invisibility, skills under change. The "camping question"‚Äîchoosing to disconnect temporarily vs. wanting to reverse integration‚Äîis accessible and generative.

Level 2: Exploration

Moderate speculation. The smartphone comparison, generational positioning, and expertise barrier questions extend patterns thoughtfully. The Archie question is particularly rich‚Äîexpect diverse responses about what makes relationships "real."

Level 3: Deep Dive

Requires strong facilitation. "Emotional responses as signals" asks students to notice their reactions without judging them. "Strangely normal" challenges expectations about what the future should feel like. The Normalization Curve activity gives students a concrete analytical tool to apply.

The Normalization Curve Activity

Students place AI tools on the curve: Novelty ‚Üí Adoption ‚Üí Invisibility ‚Üí Dependency. Requirements: at least one tool they interact with daily, at least one they rarely notice. This makes the invisibility paradox personal and concrete. Expect productive disagreement about where different tools fall‚Äîthat disagreement is the point.

Selective Computing (3 minutes, optional)

Brief discussion of choosing tools that do less by design. Frame it as one valid orientation among many‚Äî"fatigue with bloat rather than fear of technology." The examples (notification limits, no-agent zones) make it concrete.

Synthesis (5 minutes)

Use 2-3 of the synthesis questions:

  1. Which expectations changed as you heard other perspectives?
  2. Where did the group converge on likely changes or continuities?
  3. Which areas remained uncertain or contested?
  4. Which changes felt incremental vs. structural?
Closing Frame

"Maya's life in 2035 isn't science fiction. It's recognizably continuous with today‚Äîbut the texture has shifted. That's probably what your future will feel like too: strangely normal. The goal isn't to predict exactly what will happen. It's to develop realistic expectations about change, so you can navigate it thoughtfully rather than anxiously."

Reflection Questions: Guidance

Reflection Questions Overview

These questions ask students to engage with core concepts from the lesson: the invisibility paradox, the dissolving expertise barrier, and the "strangely normal" insight about how technological change feels.

Reflection Question 1: Invisibility

Prompt: The lesson argues that AI will likely become more invisible as it becomes more capable‚Äîfollowing the pattern of electricity, which went from "electric lights" to just "lights." Using examples from Maya's story, explain what this invisibility might look like in practice. What are the benefits of this pattern? What concerns does this raise about control, accountability, and choice?

Example Strong Response:

"In Maya's story, invisibility shows up everywhere. Her alarm adjusts based on her sleep cycle‚Äîshe just knows she feels less groggy. Her calendar suggests optimal times‚Äîshe accepts most without thinking about the analysis behind them. She 'barely notices' the drone notification. Even Archie, which she actively interacts with, feels like 'infrastructure' rather than a distinct tool. The benefits are clear: less friction, less cognitive load, capabilities she doesn't have to think about. But there are concerns too. When the calendar suggests her schedule, is she still choosing, or is she accepting defaults set by systems she doesn't understand? When technology becomes invisible, we lose awareness of how it shapes our choices. We can't question or adjust what we don't notice. And if something goes wrong‚Äîif the system is biased or makes errors‚Äîinvisibility makes it harder to identify and fix. Maya's world is more convenient but potentially less controllable."

What strong responses include:

  • Specific examples from Maya's story (alarm, calendar, drone notification, Archie as infrastructure)
  • Recognition of benefits: reduced friction, cognitive load, accessible capabilities
  • Recognition of concerns: loss of awareness, reduced control, difficulty identifying problems
  • Understanding that this is a pattern, not unique to AI

What weaker responses miss:

  • Vague claims without specific examples from Maya's story
  • One-sided analysis (only benefits or only concerns)
  • Missing the deeper point: invisibility means we stop noticing how technology shapes behavior and choices

Reflection Question 2: Expertise Barrier

Prompt: Maya creates a documentary about her grandmother without learning editing software‚Äîshe provides creative direction while a computer-use agent handles technical execution. The lesson suggests that "the expertise barrier that once separated amateurs from professionals has largely dissolved." If this becomes common across many fields, what becomes more valuable? What becomes less valuable? Use the concept of authentic voice from Chapter 7 in your answer.

Example Strong Response:

"When computer-use agents handle technical execution, the skills that become less valuable are the technical ones‚Äîknowing which buttons to click, which menus to navigate, which settings to adjust. Maya doesn't know where the menus are. She doesn't need to. What becomes more valuable is everything the agent can't provide: vision, intent, judgment about what matters, and authentic connection to the material. Maya's documentary isn't valuable because of its technical polish‚Äîanyone can have that now. It's valuable because it's her grandmother, her family's story, her perspective on what it means. This connects directly to authentic voice from Chapter 7. When production quality is no longer scarce, what becomes scarce is genuine stakes, lived experience, and specific perspective. The democratization of technical capability doesn't make human contribution worthless‚Äîit reveals what human contribution actually is: not execution, but meaning."

What strong responses include:

  • Recognition that technical execution skills become less valuable
  • Understanding that vision, intent, judgment, and authentic connection become more valuable
  • Clear connection to Chapter 7's authentic voice concept
  • Recognition that Maya's documentary is valuable because of her perspective, not polish

What weaker responses miss:

  • Claiming technical skills become completely "worthless" (they still matter for building systems)
  • Missing the authentic voice connection‚Äîtreating this as purely economic
  • Failing to recognize that judgment about what to create and why it matters remains human

Reflection Question 3: Strangely Normal

Prompt: Maya concludes that her life feels "strangely normal"‚Äîbetter in some ways than her parents' at her age, worse in others, about the same in many. She doesn't feel like she's living in a science fiction movie. What does this insight suggest about how technological change actually feels to the people living through it? How might this change your expectations about your own future?

Example Strong Response:

"Maya's insight suggests that dramatic change and felt normalcy can coexist. Her life includes AI companions, computer-use agents, drone deliveries, cancer detected by pattern recognition‚Äîstuff that would seem like science fiction to someone from 2015. But to her, it's just Tuesday. The changes happened gradually, each one feeling like a small adjustment rather than a revolution. This is probably how technological change always feels from the inside: you're not living in 'the future,' you're living in your life, with its ordinary routines and frustrations. This changes my expectations in two ways. First, I shouldn't expect my future to feel dramatic or revolutionary‚Äîit will probably feel normal, even if it looks very different from today. Second, I shouldn't underestimate how much can change while still feeling ordinary. Maya's world has shifted significantly, but she barely notices because she's adapted incrementally. The future isn't something that arrives; it's something you gradually become accustomed to."

What strong responses include:

  • Recognition that dramatic change and felt normalcy can coexist
  • Understanding that "the future" is always someone's present, with ordinary routines
  • Recalibration of expectations: less "everything transforms" or "nothing changes"‚Äîmore "texture shifts while life continues"
  • Connection to gradual adaptation: change accumulates without feeling revolutionary

What weaker responses miss:

  • Treating "strangely normal" as entirely reassuring or entirely disappointing without nuance
  • Missing the implication: their own future will probably feel ordinary even if it looks very different
  • Failing to connect this insight to how they might approach uncertainty

Note on Assessment

This lesson invites genuine speculation and reflection. Accept thoughtful responses even if students' expectations differ from Maya's scenario. The goal is realistic thinking about change, not agreement with any particular prediction.

Potential Challenges

Challenge: Students dismiss Maya's story as unrealistic

Response: "Which parts feel unrealistic? Let's examine them. Ten years ago, did you have a voice assistant, AI autocomplete, personalized recommendations? Many 'predictions' in Maya's story are extrapolations of things that already exist. The lesson acknowledges uncertainty‚Äîsome things may go faster, slower, or differently. But the question isn't whether Maya's exact scenario will happen; it's whether thinking through concrete possibilities helps us engage more thoughtfully than abstract speculation."

Challenge: Students are anxious about the future Maya describes

Response: Acknowledge the feeling, then redirect to agency. "What specifically concerns you? Let's look at it. Maya has concerns too‚Äîabout inequality, about access, about what it means that Archie knows her so well. But she's also living her life, making choices, reading paper books before bed. The lesson isn't saying everything will be fine; it's saying you'll still be a person navigating your life, with choices about how to engage."

Challenge: Students focus only on negative possibilities

Response: "The uncertainties section lists things that could go either direction. Maya's healthcare caught her cancer early‚Äîthat's genuinely good, even if access remains uneven. Her work week is shorter. Air quality improved. What we're practicing is holding both: real improvements alongside persistent problems. That's more honest than either optimism or pessimism alone."

Challenge: The Archie discussion becomes heated

Response: This is one of the more personal topics. If students have strong reactions (positive or negative) about AI companions, redirect to the analytical frame: "What makes Maya's distinction‚Äî'useful and company, but not real'‚Äîinteresting or problematic? What does her mother's skepticism reveal? The question isn't whether Archie is good or bad; it's what these relationships might look like and how we'll navigate them."

Challenge: Students struggle with the Normalization Curve activity

Response: Model one example. "Where would you place spam filters? Most of us rarely think about them‚Äîthey're invisible infrastructure. But we depend on them. That's probably 'Invisibility' or 'Dependency.' Where would you place AI chatbots? Still novel for many people, actively adopted by others. Different people might place them differently‚Äîand that disagreement is productive."

Challenge: Students want to know what will "really" happen

Response: "Nobody knows. That's the honest answer. The point of Maya's story isn't to predict; it's to make speculation concrete enough to engage with. The uncertainties section names what we don't know. What we're practicing is thinking realistically about change‚Äîneither assuming everything transforms nor assuming nothing does."

Challenge: Running out of time

Response: Prioritize: (1) Maya's story or key portions‚Äîthis is the anchor; (2) One discussion level with a few questions; (3) Brief synthesis. The invisibility paradox opening and selective computing section can be condensed or skipped. Field journal prompts can be completed as homework.
